## Objection: Switching LIMS Is Too Disruptive

**Renat:** My first and main question is how to deal with the objection that people don't want to change their LIMS system. It takes a lot of effort for them. What I have in mind is showing them something they can start using today — maybe functionality your competitors don't have — and then step by step they migrate to your system. Is that possible?

**Andrew (client):** One year ago that was our first idea: a foot-in-the-door strategy, selling something simpler and less expensive than LIMS. We have an intelligence layer in our system called Lab Reports — an analytics module that analyzes tests ordered and identifies under-tested patients, so the laboratory can notify physicians who then prescribe additional tests. Good for the patient, good for the lab's testing volumes. We tried to promote this for a year, but there's no demand for it right now. That's the only foot-in-the-door product we have.

We also provide IT support — we can customize workflow setups in laboratories, connect additional instruments and EMR systems, do cybersecurity audits to help labs with compliance. But that doesn't resonate either — the IT outsourcing market in the US has at least 100 competitors. So we got our focus back on the LIMS itself: it shows real traction. We have one client, demos running, negotiations ongoing. People generally like the system.

**Chris (client):** I agree with Andrew. To Renat's point, that's our only entry-level product. The common objection is it takes too much effort to switch. The response is something along the lines of: "I completely understand, we hear that all the time. Most labs tell us it's going to be disruptive and take six to twelve months. But what we've found is the process becomes much easier when the migration is structured and correctly supported from our side from the beginning. Our team handles the majority of the heavy lifting — workflow mapping, integrations, data migration, training, onboarding — to minimize as much disruption as possible."

## Why Labs Switch / Competitive Landscape

**Renat:** Why did your existing clients switch? That's the most important part of cold outreach — highlighting specific points, not one generic "we're better." What specific problems do current systems have?

**Chris:** There are really two or three major reasons.

Number one is **scalability**. As labs grow, they outgrow their existing systems. It slows down and causes internal operational issues.

Number two is **legacy on-premise systems**. These were installed ten-plus years ago when that was the only option available. Like a car from 1985 — you can only fix it so many times before you need to replace it. As you fix one thing, something else breaks. Everything slows down and causes a ton of operational issues. Do some people have concerns about security in cloud-based systems? Of course. But today that's not really a common objection anymore. For the most part it's just legacy lock-in.

**Renat:** What does the competitive landscape look like?

**Andrew:** Very fragmented. At least twenty solutions you can easily find via Google. I would say there are three to five really big companies with million-dollar revenue — Labware, LabVantage, for example. Middle-segment players: CaliberLIMS, Sapiens — turnovers around five million. Small solutions: Creepy LIMS, LIMS ABC — we've seen one of these at a prospect's site and know it can't scale. And there's another category: we've met at least three to five clients who have self-written systems. They pay eight hundred dollars to some developer who builds and maintains a custom system. It's cheap and flexible, but it doesn't scale.

## Battle Cards Strategy

**Renat:** We can create battle cards — specific per-competitor limitations and why you're better. We've done this for a CRM project for property managers: we scraped data to identify which CRM each company was using, then built specific objection-handling messages at the feature level — what they're angry about in their current system and how our client was better at those specific things. It worked because we talked about very specific pain points.

Is it possible to identify which LIMS a lab is using from public information on their website?

**Chris:** There is typically a way. A lot of labs we'll be targeting have a physician portal in the top right corner of their website. When you click that link, it takes you to a login screen that's iframed inside their existing website. You right-click, inspect the element, and see where it's hosted.

**Renat:** Vacancies can also indicate which software they use. Also, there's a review resource — I've collected four hundred fifty reviews and extracted the top six pain points for LIMS. There's some information on Reddit as well.

**Andrew:** The challenge with competitive intel is the only real way is to act like a mystery shopper — schedule a demo with competitors and make up a story. Getting detailed information from a landing page or a demo call is very limited. One option is paid interviews: we pay users a hundred-dollar Amazon gift card for their time, ask deep questions about their current system's limitations, and potentially convert them to our clients.

**Andrew:** We could also apply the same technique to identify what systems labs are using. If it's not one of the known market solutions, they probably have something custom-built or on-premise. In that case we use that as a hook to invite them to a demo.

## Target Segments

**Andrew:** We've identified ten to twelve segments. For example:
- Toxicology labs
- Anatomic pathology labs
- **Newly opened labs** — no existing LIMS yet, easiest to sell
- **Labs with Russian-speaking owners** — when they hear about In Vitro, the conversation starts very smoothly. There are at least a few dozen diagnostic labs in the US with Russian origins.

**How to identify newly opened labs:**
- **CLIA database** — has the opening date
- **NPI database** — may have additional date information
- **New domain or website registration** in the last twelve months — labs sometimes register domains before they start operating
- **Multiple open roles** — lab director, technologist, etc. — indirectly indicates a lab is about to open
- **Google Maps** — they must have a physical location since they handle actual blood work

**Renat:** For small labs — target those with ten employees or less, they likely don't have a LIMS yet. Google Maps is more accurate for headcount than LinkedIn — most small lab employees aren't active on LinkedIn.

**Andrew:** CLIA certification types matter. Basic test certifications mean the lab probably doesn't need a sophisticated LIMS. Specialized tests — it's a compliance necessity, they have to track all materials. We'll share the filtered database, not just the raw data.

## Lab Types to Target

**Andrew:** Three categories of laboratories:

1. **Physician Office Laboratories (POL)** — a physician with a small in-house lab. Priority: easier buying process, faster implementation.

2. **Independent / walk-in labs** — "Patient Service Centers" (PSC) — labs where patients walk in to give blood or other biological material. The In Vitro model.

3. **Reference labs** — accumulate orders from smaller labs that can't run certain tests themselves, run the tests, send results back to doctors and doctors and patients. We have a pilot project with one such lab.

**Not targeting hospitals** — they use EMR/EHR ecosystems like Epic and Cerner that bundle everything. Integrating with those is extremely expensive, and those systems sell it as a bundle that keeps everyone locked inside their ecosystem. A small private clinic with its own lab is a target, though.

## Russian-Speaking Segment Messaging

**Chris:** Using the In Vitro story — two thousand locations, up to one million tests per day — works very well with Russian-speaking lab owners. But with small US labs it creates two problems: "they sound too big and too complex for us," and when they hear about Russian founders, HIPAA and data compliance concerns come up immediately. So we need to be very cognizant of how we handle that messaging per segment.

When speaking to a large laboratory with Russian owners, especially in New Jersey, it lands perfectly — no data concerns. When speaking to a small lab in Florida or Alabama, the Russian origins story gives us objections we have to overcome before we even get to the substance of what we're discussing.

**Solution:** Anonymize the In Vitro case study for small labs — present the volume and locations without attribution. Use the Russian origin story only with Russian-speaking owners. Andrew handles the Russian-language outreach.

Andrew is also currently working on a case study with a startup laboratory, which will give us a different type of content for smaller labs.
